The Telescope

Physical Description and Characteristics

Gold-plated beryllium mirror array (Source)

The unique feature of JWST is its primary mirror array composed of separate segments which combine to a diameter of about 6.5 meters. Each mirror segment is made of lightweight gold-plated beryllium.

Each mirror has a mass of 20.1 kg alone and 39.48 kg with the primary mirror segment assembly (PMSA). In total, the primary mirror has a mass of 705 kg and the entire telescope approx 6200 kg, including observatory, on-orbit consumables and launch vehicle adaptor.

At the core of the telescope is the Integrated Science Instrument Module (ISIM), a rigid frame housing the electronics systems and the four science instruments or ISIM.

These components include the Near-Infrared Camera, or NIRCam, the Near-Infrared Spectrograph, or NIRSpec, the Mid-Infrared Instrument, or MIRI, and the Fine Guidance Sensor/ Near InfraRed Imager and Slitless Spectrograph , or FGS/NIRISS.

Where is it?

Construction of the telescope began at the Northrop Grumman Space Technology facility in Redondo Beach, California and most assembly has taken place within a clean room at the Goddard Space Flight Center in Greenbelt, Maryland where the telescope is currently located.

While the actual telescope is kept under close watch, you can see a full-scale model traveling around the country.

How much did it cost?

While the first realistic budget estimates were that the observatory would cost $1.6 billion and launch in 2011, the project has suffered several cost overruns and delays with current objectives aimed at a 2018 launch; more recent estimates put the cost of development and five years of operation at $8.835 billion.

As of September 2011, NASA had spent approximately $3.5B on Webb, to which the House of Representatives voted to terminate funding; however, in November 2011, Congress reversed plans to cancel the JWST and instead capped additional funding to complete the project at $8 billion.

The $8.835B cost to NASA encompasses a production cost of $7.998B, and an additional $837M for five years of operations and 2 additional years of data analysis.

In 2005, the life-cycle cost of the project was estimated at about US$4.5 billion. The ESA was to contribute about €300 million, including the launch, and the Canadian Space Agency about $39M Canadian. The primary contributions from international partners are the instruments onboard the telescope, an Ariane 5 ECA launcher, and manpower to support operations

Who operates it?

The telescope will operate completely autonomously and data will be collected by representatives from all collaborating partners, including NASA, the European Space Agency, and the Canadian Space Agency
